The Production

A scientist, a thesis, and six very human guinea pigs

"Don Alfonso is a scientist bent on testing his thesis: così fan tutte — all women are like that. A group of prototype humans are pushed through a series of experiments in human behaviour. But does Alfonso have the last laugh?"

Barefoot Opera's bold new production places a question mark — and an exclamation point — firmly at the end of Mozart's title. Così fan tutte?! The "?!" says it all: this production isn't content to simply stage the opera, it demands we interrogate it. Is Don Alfonso a visionary or a manipulator? Are the lovers victims of circumstance or their own desires? And does the opera endorse its troubling premise, or quietly skewer it?

Directed by Jenny Miller, founder of Barefoot Opera and a director whose work spans theatre, dance and singing, the production strips the opera back to its human core. In the intimate Studio 1 of the Arcola Theatre, there is nowhere for the characters — or the audience — to hide.

The Story

A delicious plot — and one of opera's most delightfully wicked bets

Naples. Two young officers, Ferrando and Guglielmo, are passionately in love — Ferrando with Dorabella, Guglielmo with Fiordiligi, two sisters. They make the mistake of bragging to their cynical philosopher friend Don Alfonso about the unshakeable faithfulness of their beloveds. Alfonso raises an eyebrow. He bets them they are wrong. And so one of opera's most delicious social experiments begins.

Act I — The Experiment Begins

Alfonso convinces the officers to fake military orders. They bid their sweethearts a heartbreaking farewell and appear to sail off to war. The sisters are inconsolable. Alfonso enlists Despina, their mischievous maid, as an accomplice. The men return in elaborate disguises — as mysterious "Albanian" strangers — and immediately begin courting the other's fiancée. The sisters are outraged. Ferrando pursues Fiordiligi; Guglielmo pursues Dorabella. When the Albanians stage a theatrical "suicide by poison," Despina — disguised as a doctor — treats them with the famously absurd "Mesmer magnet." The women's resolve, however, is beginning to crack.

Act II — The Experiment Concludes

The sisters agree to be less rigid. Dorabella capitulates first, accepting Guglielmo's advances and exchanging a portrait locket. Fiordiligi resists longer — her magnificent aria "Per pietà" is a tormented plea for strength — but she too eventually surrenders to "Ferrando." The disguised officers are shattered: their own sweethearts have been faithless. Alfonso grins. Despina, now disguised as a notary, presides over mock wedding contracts. At the moment of signing, military trumpets — the men returning from "war." The disguises are stripped away. Betrayal, confusion, forgiveness? Mozart and Da Ponte leave the ending deliberately, magnificently ambiguous.

Oscar Castellino sings the role of Guglielmo, one of the two officers at the centre of the bet — a baritone role that moves from boastful swagger to comic humiliation to genuine heartbreak across the opera's two acts.

The Music

Mozart at his most sophisticated — and most subversive

Così fan tutte contains some of Mozart's most exquisitely crafted music. The opera opens with one of the most gorgeous trios in all of opera, Soave sia il vento, a farewell of heartbreaking beauty — which is entirely ironic, since the men are not really leaving. Mozart seems to have a great time undercutting his own most beautiful moments.

Among the score's most celebrated moments:

Soave sia il vento (Trio) Come scoglio (Fiordiligi) Un'aura amorosa (Ferrando) Smanie implacabili (Dorabella) Per pietà (Fiordiligi) È amore un ladroncello (Dorabella) Donne mie, la fate a tanti (Guglielmo)

Full Title
La scuola degli amanti

The full title is Così fan tutte, ossia La scuola degli amanti — "Thus do all women, or The School for Lovers." It was the third of Mozart's three collaborations with librettist Lorenzo Da Ponte, following Le nozze di Figaro (1786) and Don Giovanni (1787). Mozart died just one year later, in 1791, making Così fan tutte one of his final completed masterworks.

Did You Know?

The opera they tried to ban

  • For much of the 19th and early 20th centuries, Così fan tutte was considered morally scandalous and some opera houses substituted entirely different plots — keeping Mozart's music but inventing more "respectable" stories around it.
  • It was reportedly one of Napoleon Bonaparte's favourite operas — which perhaps says something about his views on love and loyalty.
  • The opera was so scandalous that Beethoven reportedly refused to set any Da Ponte libretti partly because of the immorality of Così — though he greatly admired Mozart's music.
  • Da Ponte wrote the librettos for all three of Mozart's greatest comic operas in a remarkable three-year burst: Figaro (1786), Don Giovanni (1787), Così fan tutte (1790).
  • The "Albanian" disguises in the opera were apparently so absurd that audiences at the premiere reportedly laughed at the sight of them — a comedy tradition Mozart fully intended.
  • The opera's ending is deliberately ambiguous: Mozart and Da Ponte never spell out whether the original couples reconcile or whether the new pairings stick. Productions have been arguing about it ever since.

About the Company

Barefoot Opera

Founded in 2011 by Jenny Miller, Barefoot Opera grew from a vision to create an ensemble style of working in which the whole group shared storytelling — with their whole bodies. The company's approach spans theatre, dance and classical singing, combining them into a distinctive performance style in which inner dancer, classical singer and actor are one.

Based in Sussex but performing throughout the South East and London, Barefoot Opera is known for accessible, intimate productions that challenge the notion that opera is elitist. They re-work the classics, commission contemporary work, and engage new audiences — bringing opera to venues and communities far beyond the traditional concert hall.

Their previous productions include Oscar Castellino's earlier role as Enrico in Lucia di Lammermoor and Belcore in L'Elisir d'Amore, making this a reunion of long-standing artistic collaboration.

About the Festival

Grimeborn Opera Festival — 19th Year

The Grimeborn Opera Festival is the UK's most beloved fringe opera festival, held every summer at Arcola Theatre in Dalston, East London. Founded in 2007 by Arcola's Artistic Director Mehmet Ergen, its name is a knowing wink at the world-famous Glyndebourne Festival in East Sussex — swapping the rolling green Sussex countryside for the congested creative energy of Hackney.

Now in its 19th year, Grimeborn 2026 runs from 15 July to 5 September, presenting 13 operas — 6 new works and 7 reimagined classics — with themes of environmental urgency, political tension and women's perspectives. Ticket prices remain deliberately accessible at £15–£39, reflecting Arcola's longstanding commitment to widening access to opera.

Arcola Theatre itself, at 24 Ashwin Street E8, is a converted textile factory that has become one of the UK's most respected fringe theatres — winner of multiple awards and the spiritual home of adventurous, community-rooted performance in East London.
